Frogs

Frogs are the most well-known amphibians in the world. When most people think of amphibians, their minds go straight to the tiny frogs hopping around a pond. But frogs are much more than the little green creatures you see in your backyard. They live all over the globe and have some of the most impressive adaptations in the animal kingdom.

Frogs are especially famous for their unique life cycle. Everything begins with their eggs, which have no shells at all. Once they hatch, the young become tadpoles that feed on aquatic plants, insects, small fish, and almost anything they can fit into their tiny mouths. As they grow, tadpoles transform into froglets, and eventually into fully developed adult frogs.

Frogs of Wisconsin

Gray Tree Frog

Most native frogs in Wisconsin prefer ponds and wetlands, but there are a few exceptions. One of the most fascinating is the gray tree frog. This species is one of the only arboreal frogs in Wisconsin, spending much of its time in the trees. Gray tree frogs can appear in many colors, including dark gray, brown, and even dusty green to blend in with tree bark.

Green Frog

Green frogs are the most common frog species in Wisconsin. They live in ponds, lakes, streams, and shallow rivers. Their diet includes flies, moths, dragonflies, mosquitoes, and other small insects. Green frogs typically stay near the water their entire lives and hibernate during the winter.

Wood Frog

Wood frogs live on the forest floor, blending into the leaf litter with their small brown bodies. Even though they do not seem like tree climbers, they have sticky toes that allow them to climb vertical surfaces to find food or escape predators.
